# StoryBrand Messaging Framework

Donald Miller's SB7: clarify your message so customers will listen. Customers don't buy the best products — they buy the ones they can understand fastest.

## Core Principle

**The customer is the hero, not your brand. Your brand is the guide who helps the hero win.** Position yourself as the hero and you compete with your customer; position yourself as the guide and you serve them.

## Scoring

Rate any copy 0-10 against SB7. State current score and changes to reach 10/10.

## The SB7 Framework

A story = a character who wants something meets a problem; a guide gives them a plan and calls them to action that ends in success and helps them avoid failure.

1. **A Character (Hero)** — define the ONE thing the customer wants (a single desire opens a story gap). Tie it to survival (physical/financial/relational/spiritual). Tailor per segment. Copy: "You want [specific desire]..."
2. **Has a Problem** — name it at three levels: external (tangible), internal (emotional — what customers actually buy solutions to), philosophical (the injustice). Personify a specific villain ("Wall Street jargon designed to confuse you"). Most brands stop at external.
3. **Meets a Guide** — your brand expresses **empathy** ("we understand what it's like to...") AND **authority** (testimonials, logos, stats, awards). Empathy without authority seems weak; authority without empathy seems arrogant. Never make your origin story the centerpiece — that's hero behavior.
4. **Gives a Plan** — a **Process Plan** (3-4 numbered steps with action verbs: "1. Book a demo. 2. Get onboarded. 3. See results.") and an **Agreement Plan** (risk-removing commitments: "Cancel anytime", "30-day money-back"). More than 4 steps overwhelms.
5. **Calls to Action** — a **Direct CTA** (prominent button: "Buy Now", "Schedule a Call", repeated down the page) plus a **Transitional CTA** (lower commitment: "Download Free Guide", "Watch Demo") that keeps not-yet-ready people in the story.
6. **Helps Avoid Failure** — show the stakes (without stakes there's no story). Humans are loss-averse. A taste of consequence/opportunity cost is enough — never a scare campaign. "Every day without [solution], you're [cost of inaction]."
7. **Ends in Success** — paint a vivid picture of life after: status ("become the go-to expert"), completeness ("financial peace of mind"), self-realization. Show transformation with before/after and specific numbers, not features. This closes the story gap from Element 1.

## Applying It
- **Homepage** lead with the desire as an outcome ("You want a beautiful smile" not "our dentistry is excellent").
- **One-liner / elevator pitch**: [character + problem] + [your plan] + [success]. "Most teams lose customers to confusing messaging. We clarify your message in three steps so customers buy."
- **Email nurture**: lead with the internal problem; reference the plan; close on success + light urgency.

## Ethics
Ground desires in real research; name real frustrations (never invent suffering); claim only earned authority (real testimonials, accurate stats); promise only outcomes you reliably deliver; state proportionate consequences of inaction — no fear-mongering.
