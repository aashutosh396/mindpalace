# Interview Synthesis

Transform raw user-research data into structured insights that drive product decisions. A good synthesis goes beyond listing what people said — it finds patterns across conversations, connects observations to underlying needs, and translates findings into actionable recommendations.

## When to use

- After a round of user interviews (typically 5+ participants)
- Following customer discovery calls or sales feedback sessions
- After usability testing, to consolidate observations
- When stakeholders need a research summary
- Before ideation, to ground the team in user reality

## When NOT to use

- Summarizing one internal meeting for attendees → meeting recap
- Patterns across many meetings over time → meeting synthesis
- Data is survey responses, not interviews → survey analysis
- Findings ready and you're framing the problem → problem statement

## How

1. **Gather raw material.** Notes/transcripts/recordings from ≥3 participants; record research objective and method.
2. **Create participant profiles.** Role, segment, tenure, notable traits — lets readers judge representativeness.
3. **Identify recurring themes.** Tag observations by topic; surface themes appearing across 3+ participants; separate recurring from one-off.
4. **Extract meaningful quotes.** 3-5 verbatim per theme, attributed to participant IDs; pick specific/emotional/articulate ones.
5. **Synthesize into insights.** Move from observation ("users mentioned X") to interpretation ("users need Y because Z").
6. **Formulate recommendations.** Prioritized, each tied to an insight, with a confidence level by strength of evidence.
7. **Document limitations.** What you didn't learn, sample biases, areas needing more research.

## Output sections

Research Overview; Key Themes; Notable Quotes; Insights; Recommendations; Appendix.

## Quality checklist

- [ ] Themes backed by 3+ participants
- [ ] Quotes verbatim and attributed (IDs, no PII)
- [ ] Insights explain "why", not just "what"
- [ ] Recommendations specific and actionable
- [ ] Limitations and biases acknowledged
