# Hard-Call — Framework for Decisions With No Good Options

These decisions don't have a right answer — only a less wrong one. They're hard because real people are affected, you've been avoiding it (so it's worse), it's often irreversible, and your judgment is clouded by your feelings. **Most hard decisions are late decisions** — the cost of delay almost always exceeds the cost of the decision.

## The Framework

**1. Reversibility test.** Reversible (try/learn/adjust) / partially reversible (painful but possible) / irreversible (can't undo — layoff, shutdown with lock-in). Irreversible → higher certainty bar, more diligence. If you're treating a reversible decision as irreversible, you're avoiding it.

**2. 10/10/10.** How will you feel 10 minutes from now? 10 months? 10 years? The 10-minute feeling is least reliable; the 10-year view clarifies the right call. Most hard decisions look obvious at 10 years — the question is tolerating the 10-minute pain.

**3. Andy Grove test.** "If we got replaced tomorrow, what would a new CEO do?" Fresh eyes, no sunk cost. If the answer is clear to an outsider, ask: why haven't you done it?

**4. Stakeholder impact map.** Per option, map affected employees / remaining team / customers / investors / you. Not to find the option that hurts nobody (none exists) — to see the full picture.

**5. Pre-announcement test.** Write the announcement before deciding. If you can't write it, you're not ready. It forces you to confront the reality and surfaces whether your reasoning holds.

**6. Communication plan.** Who knows first (the person affected, before anyone else); how (in person for personal impact, never email); what you'll say (honest, direct, compassionate); what they can ask; what comes next.

## Decision-Specific Rules
- **Firing a co-founder** — performance vs values problem? Have you been *explicit* (not hinted)? Cap-table/legal? If you've thought about it 3+ months, you already know — the question is when, not whether.
- **Layoffs** — one-time reset or start of decline? Cutting deep enough? (Two rounds destroys trust.) Rule: cut once, cut deep, cut with dignity. Uncertainty is worse than clarity.
- **Pivoting** — true pivot or optimization? Evidence of new opportunity or running from failure? Rule: pivots are pulled by new-opportunity evidence, not pushed by current-path failure.
- **Killing a product line** — customer migration path? What do the builders do? Is "sell it"/"spin it out" better than "kill it"?

## The Avoiding-It Test
You're avoiding a hard call if: you've thought about it weekly for a month+; you're hoping it resolves itself; you're waiting for data that'll never feel enough; you've had the conversation in your head but not in real life; others have noticed. Make the call. Make it clearly. Make it with dignity.
