# Internal Narrative Builder

One company, many audiences, same truth — different lenses. Narrative inconsistency is trust erosion. The same fact lands differently by who hears it and what they need; the skill is maintaining truth while serving each audience's actual question.

## Framework

**1. Build the core narrative.** One paragraph every other communication derives from. Template: "[Company] exists to [mission — present tense, specific]. We're building [what] because [problem]. Our approach is [unique way]. We're at [honest current state] heading toward [concrete destination]." Good = usable + specific ("80 care facilities in DACH paying €800K ARR, heading to €3M"). Bad = "innovative AI company revolutionizing… across the continuum of care" (says nothing).

**2. Audience translation matrix.** Translate the core for employees / investors / customers / candidates — same facts, different frame. Rules: never contradict yourself across audiences (different framing ≠ different facts); investors and employees see each other, board talks to your team, candidates google you.

**3. Contradiction detection (before any major comms).** What did we tell investors last month about [topic]? What did we tell employees? Consistent? Common contradictions: "efficient growth" to investors + "hiring aggressively" to candidates; "strong pipeline" to investors + "sales struggling" at all-hands. When caught: fix the less-accurate version, then communicate the correction explicitly — correcting yourself first builds more trust than getting caught.

**4. Cadence.** Employees: all-hands monthly (CEO) + team weekly. Investors: written monthly (CEO+CFO). Board: quarterly. Customers: per release. Candidates: ongoing. Partners: quarterly QBR.

**5. All-hands.** Lead with honest state (no spin), connect company performance to individual work, give a reason to be proud, real Q&A (not curated). Failure modes: CEO speaks 55/60 min; all good news; metrics without context; deflected questions never followed up.

**6. Crisis communication.** **The 4-hour rule:** if something is public or about to be, communicate internally within 4 hours — employees should never learn company news from Twitter. Sequence (hour 0-4 internal first): acknowledge factually → what you know / don't know yet → what you're doing → what to do if asked → when you'll update. External (4-24h) only if public, consistent with internal, legal-reviewed if liability. Never: silence, spin, "no comment", blaming.

## Consistency Checklist (before external comms)
Consistent with last month's investor update? With the last all-hands? Contradicts website/careers/press? Would an employee recognize the company described? Would an investor find your internal deck inconsistent? Accurate about current state or projecting aspiration?

## Red Flags
Departments describe the mission differently; investor narrative = growth + employee = stability; all-hands mostly one-way slides; screened Q&A; bad news reaches employees via Slack rumors first; careers page describes a culture employees don't recognize.
