# Strategy Toolkit

Sun Tzu's insight: "The victorious warrior wins first and then goes to war." Strategy is what happens before the contest — creating conditions (position, intelligence, timing, alliances) so the outcome is shaped before it begins. Draws on Sun Tzu, Clausewitz, Musashi, and competitive theory. Not about aggression — about reading competitive contexts clearly and acting with economy, intelligence, and timing.

## When to use

Competitive and adversarial contexts: market positioning, negotiation, contests under resource constraints. For purely cooperative situations where parties share objectives, use decision or systems thinking instead.

## Route to the right play

First confirm the actor, the opponent/competitive pressure, and the objective in one sentence. Then present the 3–4 most relevant plays for the specific situation (not all eight), one sentence each on fit, and execute on selection.

- **Terrain** — Maps available positions, favorable and dangerous ground; which battles are worth fighting. *Usually the right first move in a complex situation.*
- **Intelligence** — Audits what you know vs what you assume about yourself and the opponent; closes the gaps that cause strategic failure.
- **Timing** — Conditions favoring action vs patience; reads the opponent's rhythm; identifies trigger conditions for the right moment.
- **Force Economy** — Finds the minimum intervention that achieves the objective; identifies leverage points where small input yields large output. *For when you're outgunned.*
- **Positioning** — Builds conditions for unassailability before the contest: what must be true for a rational opponent to choose a different arena.
- **Deception** — Manages information asymmetry in legitimate competition; protects your position; surfaces what the opponent may be concealing.
- **Victory** — Defines what winning actually means before the contest; prevents the pyrrhic trap of winning in ways that lose the larger goal.
- **Alliance** — Maps parties, natural allies, swing parties, alliance stability; alliances built on shared interest, not goodwill alone.

## Notes

- If the objective itself is unclear, start with **Victory** — you cannot choose terrain, timing, or force economy without knowing what winning means.
- If the situation is genuinely complex, **Terrain** is usually the right first move: it establishes the ground on which all other choices operate.
