# Influence Psychology Framework

Cialdini's science of persuasion applied ethically to products, copy, and sales — the principles behind why people say "yes."

## Core Principle

People don't decide rationally — they use mental shortcuts (heuristics) that can be triggered. These evolved because they're usually reliable. Understanding them lets you design experiences that align with how people actually decide. Use ethically — to align with genuine value, never to manipulate.

## The Seven Principles

### 1. Reciprocity
People feel obligated to give back to those who gave first. The gift must come before the request; unexpected/personalized beats generic; even small gifts create obligation. Apply: free trials (full access then ask), value-first content, two-sided referral rewards. Ethics: give genuine value, don't manufacture artificial debt.

### 2. Commitment & Consistency
People want to be consistent with past statements and actions. Small commitments lead to larger ones (foot-in-the-door); public > private, written > verbal, active > passive. Onboarding sequence: micro-commitment ("What brings you here?") → small action → public/written goal → reinforce ("Based on what you told us..."). Ethics: allow easy reversibility — don't lock people into commitments they didn't freely make.

### 3. Social Proof
People decide what's correct by seeing what others do — strongest when uncertain and when others are similar. Specific numbers beat vague ("2,347 users" > "thousands"). Negative social proof backfires ("9 of 10 don't..."). Types: wisdom of crowds, wisdom of friends, expert, celebrity, certification, user/similar-success. Ethics: never fabricate — real numbers, real testimonials, disclose curation.

### 4. Authority
People follow credible experts; titles, credentials, even symbols trigger compliance. Admitting a weakness paradoxically increases trustworthiness — lead with it before strengths. Sources: credentials, experience, association (investors/partners), original content/research, transparency about limitations. Ethics: claim only real, relevant expertise (it doesn't transfer across domains).

### 5. Liking
People say yes to those they like. Drivers: similarity, genuine compliments, cooperation toward shared goals, attractiveness, familiarity. Apply: relatable founder stories, matching customer language, friendly human tone. Ethics: build real rapport, not manufactured flattery.

### 6. Scarcity
People want more of what's running out or restricted. Strongest for genuinely limited supply, time-bound offers, and exclusive access; loss framing ("don't miss out") beats gain framing. Apply: real low-stock indicators, genuine deadlines, limited cohorts. Ethics: scarcity must be real — fake countdowns and false "only 2 left" destroy trust.

### 7. Unity
People say yes to those they consider part of "us" — shared identity, not just similarity. Apply: community membership, shared-mission language ("we builders..."), co-creation, in-group naming. Ethics: foster genuine belonging, not tribal manipulation.

## Combining Principles
The strongest experiences stack several: a free guide (reciprocity) from a credentialed founder (authority) showing 10,000 similar users' results (social proof) for a limited cohort (scarcity). Always ground each in real value.

## Overall Ethical Boundary
Every principle can persuade or manipulate. The test: does the trigger help the user make a decision that genuinely serves them? If it only serves your metrics at the user's expense, it's a dark pattern — don't ship it.
