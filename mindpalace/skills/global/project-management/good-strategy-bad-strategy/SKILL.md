# Good Strategy Bad Strategy

Richard Rumelt's framework for creating and auditing strategy. Good strategy = honest diagnosis + guiding policy + coherent action — not goals, vision, or wishful thinking.

## Core Principle

**Strategy is coherent action backed by an honest diagnosis.** A goal ("20% growth") names an ambition; strategy explains how, given the real obstacles. Bad strategy is an active substitute for strategy: buzzword fluff, refusing to name the challenge, laundry lists of initiatives. The heart of strategy is choice — concentrating effort on the one or two pivotal objectives that unlock everything else.

## Scoring

Rate any strategy/plan/deck 0-10: 9-10 complete kernel aimed at a pivot point with an explicit no-list; 5-6 challenge named but plan is independent initiatives with goals masquerading as strategy; 0-2 pure fluff and denial of the real challenge. State score and fixes.

## 1. The Kernel
Every good strategy has three parts:
- **Diagnosis** — defines and simplifies the critical challenge (often by analogy). This is the pivot; reframing it changes everything downstream.
- **Guiding Policy** — the overall approach that rules out vast classes of action. Not a goal/vision. If a competitor could paste it into their deck unchanged, it's a platitude. A real one feels like a choice with losers.
- **Coherent Actions** — coordinated, resource-backed steps that reinforce each other; each has an owner, budget, date. Most plans fail by jumping from ambition straight to a list of independent initiatives.

A kernel needs no mission/vision/values preamble and fits on one page.

## 2. Detecting Bad Strategy (four hallmarks)
- **Fluff** — restate plainly; "customer-centric intermediation" collapses to "we are a bank." Negation test: if the opposite is absurd ("we will not be customer focused"), it carries no information.
- **Failure to face the challenge** — if the obstacle is never named, the strategy can't be evaluated.
- **Mistaking goals for strategy** — "20% growth" is a goal; exhortation is motivation, not a lever.
- **Bad objectives** — dog's-dinner laundry lists (47 "strategies") or blue-sky restatements of the end state.

Causes: unwillingness to choose, template (vision-mission-values) planning, positive-thinking culture. Audit the document, not the people.

## 3. Sources of Power
Apply strength where it has greatest effect:
- **Leverage** = anticipation × pivot point × concentration. Commit past the threshold where results become visible.
- **Proximate objectives** — close enough to actually hit; under ambiguity choose closer targets.
- **Chain-link systems** — capped by the weakest link; fix it before scaling. A fully matched chain is also the deepest moat.
- **Design** — premeditated coordination of parts; pays when stakes are high and resources scarce.
- **Focus** and **using advantage** — deepen asymmetries, strengthen isolating mechanisms (network effects, brand, patents, tacit know-how).

## 4. Dynamics & Inertia
Waves of change (tech shifts, deregulation, demographics) redistribute advantage and are the attacker's friend. Incumbents are held back by inertia (routine, culture, proxy) and entropy (drift into blur). Guideposts: rising fixed costs, rule changes, predictable biases, incumbent margin-defense, attractor states ("who does the work, who gets paid in the end state?"). Exploit a rival's inertia where responding forces them to break their own economics.

## 5. Think Like a Strategist
Strategy is a hypothesis, not a deduction from goals. Diagnose → formulate → test against evidence → revise. Techniques: **create-destroy** (attack your own front-runner as hard as a rival's), **virtual panel of experts** (simulate respected critics), **written first-person kernel** with an explicit no-list. First conclusions are the enemy — ask "what else could be going on?"

## Quick Diagnostic
Does it name the critical challenge? Is there a guiding policy that rules out whole classes of action ("therefore we will not...")? Are actions coordinated and resource-backed? Would it be wrong for your nearest competitor (else it's generic)? Is the first objective close enough to hit? Does it exploit a wave/asymmetry/rival inertia? Is there an explicit no-list? Has anyone tried to destroy it before adopting?
