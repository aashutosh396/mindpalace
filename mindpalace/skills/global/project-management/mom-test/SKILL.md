# The Mom Test Framework

Rob Fitzpatrick's rules for customer conversations that won't lie to you. Everyone is lying — not maliciously, but because you're asking the wrong questions. Ask questions so good even your mom can't give false validation.

## Core Principle

**Good conversations are about their life, not your idea.** The moment you mention what you're building, people switch from truth to politeness. Talk about their problems and existing behavior, ask about specifics in the past (not hypotheticals), and talk less — aim for them speaking 80%+ of the time.

## Scoring

Rate any conversation 0-10: a 10 focuses on the customer's life and past behavior, no leading, no pitching, clear commitment signals. State score and fixes.

## Framework

### 1. The Three Rules
- Talk about their life, not your idea — never mention your solution until the end, if at all.
- Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or hypotheticals about the future.
- Talk less, listen more. A question fails the Mom Test if the answer is always "yes" regardless of whether the business succeeds.

### 2. Good vs Bad Questions
- Bad: "Do you think it's a good idea?" / "Would you buy X?" / "How much would you pay?" → hypothetical, always anchored to please you.
- Good: "How are you dealing with this today?" / "What have you tried and why did you stop?" / "Where does the budget for this come from?"
- The scariest questions — the ones that could change what you're building — give the most useful data.

### 3. Avoid Compliments, Fluff, Ideas
Three types of bad data feel like progress but mislead: **compliments** ("great idea!"), **fluff** (hypotheticals, "I always/never"), **feature ideas** disconnected from real problems. Deflect compliments immediately: "Thanks — but what does your current process look like?" Dig into the motivation behind every feature request. Symptom of a bad conversation: you leave feeling great with no concrete facts.

### 4. Commitment & Advancement
The currency is commitment, not compliments. End every conversation with a clear advance or a clear "no" — the worst outcome is a polite zombie lead. Commitment currencies: **time** (meeting, trial), **reputation** (intro, testimonial), **money** (deposit, pre-order, LOI). Know your "ask" before the meeting. A "no" beats a "maybe." If they won't give time, they won't give money.

### 5. Finding Conversations
Best conversations happen casually — warm intros, events, communities, coffee. Formal "customer interview" framing triggers performance mode. "I'm trying to learn about the industry, can I buy you coffee?" beats "Can I interview you?" Warm intros are the best source.

### 6. Processing & Learning
Conversations only count if processed. Take notes during/immediately after; separate facts (what they said/did) from interpretations; share raw notes with the team, not filtered summaries. Update three beliefs after each batch: the problem, the segment, the solution. Stop talking and start building when conversations start repeating.

## Common Mistakes
- Pitching instead of asking about their life → don't mention your idea until the end.
- "Would you buy this?" → ask what they already spend/do.
- Accepting compliments as validation → deflect, ask what they do today.
- Talking too much → they should talk 80%+.
- No clear ask → know your advance (trial, intro, pre-order) beforehand.
- Formal interviews → keep it casual.
- Not processing notes as a team → share raw notes weekly, update shared beliefs.

## Quick Diagnostic
Did it focus on their life and past behavior (not your idea)? Get concrete facts about what they've already done? Get a commitment (time/reputation/money)? Did they do most of the talking? Learn something that could change what you build? Update your beliefs? Can you summarize facts (not opinions)?
