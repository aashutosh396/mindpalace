# Jobs to Be Done Framework

Christensen's innovation framework: customers don't buy products — they "hire" them to make progress in specific circumstances.

## Core Principle

**Job to Be Done = the progress a customer wants in specific circumstances.** Progress (not goal, not solution), circumstances (context defines the job, demographics don't), hiring/firing (customers actively choose).

## Scoring

Rate strategy/positioning 0-10 against these principles. State current score and fixes to 10/10.

## Three Dimensions
Every job has all three — omit any and it fails: **Functional** (what they need to do), **Emotional** (how they want to feel), **Social** (how they want to be perceived). Milkshake: occupy a boring commute + small treat for myself + seen as a sensible parent.

## Framework

### 1. Job Statement
Format: **"When [circumstances], I want to [progress], so I can [outcome]."** Never mentions your product or any solution. Jobs are stable; solutions change. Use in ideation, feature prioritization, and copy (lead with the situation, not the product category).

### 2. Forces of Progress
Switching happens only when **Push + Pull > Habit + Anxiety**. Push ("this annoys me"), Pull ("I want this"), Habit ("always done it this way"), Anxiety ("what if it fails?"). Most teams only increase Pull and ignore the anti-change forces — reducing Anxiety (trials, guarantees, social proof) and Habit (one-click migration) is often more effective.

### 3. Big Hire vs Little Hire
**Big Hire** = purchase/signup (once, driven by marketing/onboarding). **Little Hire** = decision to actually use it (repeated, driven by product quality/UX). Retention problems are almost always Little Hire failures — bought but never used. Track first-use and weekly-active separately from signup conversion.

### 4. Non-Obvious Competition
True competitors = everything hireable for the same job, across categories. A milkshake competes with bananas, bagels, podcasts. **Non-consumption (doing nothing) is often the biggest competitor.** Workarounds reveal unserved jobs. Position against the real alternative ("doing it manually"), and price against the job's value, not similar products.

### 5. Discovery Interviews
Don't ask "what do you need" — reconstruct the purchase timeline: First thought (what was happening, what frustrated you?) → Search (what alternatives, what eliminated them?) → Purchase (where were you, what convinced you, what were you afraid of?) → Usage (meeting expectations? what's missing?). Watch for workarounds, non-consumption, compensating behaviors. Use verbatim customer language in copy.

### 6. Design for the Job
Build features, metrics, and teams around the job, not internal capabilities. Replace satisfaction/NPS with "did the job get done?" and "reasons for hiring and firing." Every feature proposal must name the job dimension it serves; if it can't, you don't understand the job yet.

## Common Mistakes
- Defining jobs around your product → define from the customer's view, never naming your product.
- Functional-only jobs → always complete all three dimensions.
- Confusing jobs with goals (too abstract) or tasks (too specific) → jobs = progress in circumstances.
- Only increasing Pull → design interventions for Anxiety and Habit.
- Winning Big Hire, losing Little Hire → optimize repeated usage separately.
- Asking "what do you want?" → use timeline interviews of real behavior.
- Defining competition by category → map every alternative hire, including doing nothing.

## Quick Diagnostic
Can you state the job in one sentence without naming your product? Mapped all four forces? Know the emotional + social dimensions? Identified non-obvious competitors? Tracking Little Hire separately? Can the team explain how each feature serves the job? Interviewed 10+ customers on their purchase timeline?
