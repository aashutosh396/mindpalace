# Product Positioning Framework

April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome" method: define how your product is the best in the world at delivering something a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about.

## Core Principle

**Positioning is not messaging — it is context.** It defines what category customers place you in, what alternatives they compare you to, and how they judge your value. Customers always evaluate relative to alternatives; deliberately choose the context that makes your strengths obvious. Get it wrong and no clever copy saves you.

## Scoring

Rate positioning 0-10: 0-2 customers can't explain what it is; 5-6 some components clear but team describes it differently; 9-10 every component reinforces the others and customers instantly get what/why-different/why-care. State score and fixes.

## The 5-Step Process

### Step 1: Competitive Alternatives
What would your best customers use if you vanished? Ask happy customers (not prospects). The most common alternative is often a spreadsheet, a manual process, or "do nothing" — not a competitor. Group similar alternatives; segments may differ.

### Step 2: Unique Attributes
List everything you have that the alternatives don't — features, architecture, business model, expertise, integrations, community. Must be both **unique AND true**. "Better/faster" isn't unique. Apply the "only we" test. Attributes are facts; benefits come next.

### Step 3: Map Attributes to Value
For each attribute apply "So what?" repeatedly until you reach a value customers care about, in their language. Chain: Feature → Advantage → Value ("real-time collaboration" → "teams work simultaneously" → "close books 3 days faster"). Cluster into 2-4 **value themes** (time, money, risk, growth, quality), each backed by proof points.

### Step 4: Best-Fit Customers
The tightest definition of who your product is perfect for right now (not your TAM). Use identifiable traits — job title, company size, industry, tech stack — not psychographics. Work backward from your happiest customers. "Everyone" is never valid. Define negative criteria (who is NOT a fit) too. Best-fit customers buy fastest, churn least, refer most.

### Step 5: Market Category
Pick the frame that makes your value obvious. Three options:
- **Head-to-head:** claim "best" in an established category — you inherit its assumptions and competitors.
- **Subcategory:** redefine a slice ("CRM for real estate") — built-in awareness, shifted criteria.
- **New category:** only when genuinely unlike anything — you pay an "education tax."
Category changes everything: competitors, evaluation criteria, pricing, buyer expectations. Test: do prospects "get it" in the first 30 seconds?

## The 10 Components
Competitive Alternatives · Unique Attributes · Value Themes · Best-Fit Customers · Market Category · Relevant Trends · Positioning Statement (internal one-liner) · Sales Narrative (problem → old way → new way → solution → proof) · Messaging (external language) · Content Strategy (what positioning tells you to create).

## Copy Patterns
- "Unlike [alternative], [product] does [unique thing]."
- "The only [category] that [unique attribute]."
- "[Value outcome] with [product], powered by [unique attribute]."
- "Built for [specific customer] who [specific situation]."

## Ethics
Base alternatives and value claims on real customer research, never assumptions. Don't claim attributes a competitor also has. Don't invent a new category just to dodge competition — only when genuinely needed.
