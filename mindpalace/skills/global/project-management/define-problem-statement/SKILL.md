# Problem Statement

A concise document that frames the problem you're solving, articulates impact on users and the business, and defines clear success criteria. It is the foundation for all later product work — ensure alignment on *what* problem to solve before jumping to *how*.

## When to use

- Starting a new initiative/project to establish shared understanding
- Realigning a drifted project back to its original intent
- Communicating up to leadership about priorities
- Evaluating whether a proposed solution actually addresses the core problem
- Onboarding new team members for context

## When NOT to use

- Problem already agreed and engineering needs the spec → PRD
- You want to propose and compare solution approaches → solution brief
- Capturing customer motivation rather than a business problem → JTBD canvas
- The "problem" is really an unvalidated assumption → frame as hypothesis and test it first

## How

1. **Identify the user segment.** Be specific (e.g. "mobile shoppers completing checkout", not "users").
2. **Understand the pain points.** Friction, frustration, unmet need; probe severity and frequency; cite research, support tickets, behavioral data.
3. **Establish business context.** Connect the user problem to revenue/retention/growth/strategy. Why now?
4. **Define success metrics.** What moves if solved; current baseline and target; specific and time-bound.
5. **Surface constraints.** Technical limits, resource constraints, regulatory needs, dependencies.
6. **Capture open questions.** What's unknown; assumptions needing validation; further research needed.

## Output sections

Problem Summary; User Impact; Business Context; Success Criteria; Constraints & Considerations; Open Questions.

## Quality checklist

- [ ] User segment specific, not vague
- [ ] Pain points evidenced (research/data), not asserted
- [ ] Business impact and "why now" clear
- [ ] Success metrics measurable with baseline + target
- [ ] Constraints and open questions captured honestly
