# SaaS Pricing Strategy

Pricing is positioning, not math. The right price sits between what the next-best alternative costs and what customers believe they get. Most SaaS is underpriced.

## Gather context
Current state (plans, price points, billing model, trial→paid conversion, ARPU, churn) · business (B2B/B2C, self-serve/sales-assisted, segments, competitors + their prices, cost-to-serve) · goals (design/optimize/increase, constraints like grandfathered customers).

## Modes
**Design from scratch** · **Optimize existing** · **Plan a price increase**.

## The three axes (get all three, in order)
1. **Value metric** — what you charge for (how it scales). Lock this FIRST.
2. **Packaging** — what's in each tier.
3. **Price point** — the number. Most teams skip straight here; that's backwards.

## Value metric selection
Per seat (collaboration/CRMs) · per usage (API/infra/AI) · per feature (platforms/add-ons) · flat fee (SMB) · per outcome · hybrid (mature SaaS). Choose by: what makes a customer pay more (= your metric) · does it scale with their success · easy to understand · hard to game. Red flags: per-seat where one power user does all the work; flat fee when some derive 10x value; per-API-call when usage swings wildly.

## Good-better-best tiers (3 is standard — it anchors perception)
- **Good (entry)** — captures the would-churn-if-higher segment; limited; NOT free; covers costs.
- **Better (default)** — push most customers here; 2-3x entry; everything a growing company needs; mark "recommended."
- **Best** — enterprise; may be "contact us"; unlocks SSO, audit logs, SLA, dedicated support, custom contracts.

## Value-based pricing
`[Cost of nothing] … [Next-best alternative] … [YOUR PRICE] … [Perceived value]`. Define the next-best alternative + its cost → estimate value delivered (time saved × rate, revenue protected, risk avoided; ask best customers "what would you lose if you stopped tomorrow?") → price ~10-20% of documented value. Don't price below the next-best alternative. **Conversion signal**: >40% trial→paid = likely underpriced (test increase); 15-30% healthy; <10% pricing high or funnel friction.

## Research methods
- **Van Westendorp PSM** — 4 questions (too cheap / bargain / expensive-but-acceptable / too expensive); plot curves; "too cheap"∩"too expensive" = acceptable range, "bargain"∩"expensive" = optimal point. n≥30.
- **MaxDiff** — relative feature value; informs packaging more than price point.
- **Competitor benchmarking** — list alternatives, record prices + inclusions, find where you over/under-deliver; price relative to positioning (premium 20-40% above, value at/below). Don't just copy competitor prices.

## Price increases (highest-ROI move; most wait too long)
Strategies: new-customers-only (low risk) · grandfather + delayed · tied to value delivery · plan restructure · uniform. Execution: quantify new MRR at 100/80/70% retention → segment by risk → 60-90d notice → communicate the specific reason → offer a path (lock-in via annual) → arm CS team → monitor 60 days. Expected churn from 20-30% increase: 5-15%; proceed if net revenue is positive.

## Pricing page
**Above fold**: plan names, price with monthly/annual toggle (annual shows savings), 3-5 differentiators/plan, CTA per plan, "most popular" badge. **Below**: full feature comparison (✅/❌), FAQ (cancel anytime / hitting limits / refunds / data security / upgrade-downgrade), social proof per tier, security badges if enterprise. Show annual by default, show savings explicitly, never hide monthly.

## Proactive flags
>40% trial→paid → underpriced, test +20-30%. All on middle tier → no upsell path. Customer wants out-of-tier features → expansion left on table. Churn >5%/mo → fix churn before raising. No change in 2+ years → inflation alone justifies +10-15%. Only one option → no anchoring; add a third tier.
